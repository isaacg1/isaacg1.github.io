Today, I'm tracing back my line of academic advisors (PhD advisor to PhD advisor) as far as I can, using the [Mathematics Genealogy](https://www.mathgenealogy.org). The longest-ago person in their database appears to be [Ibn Sina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avicenna), also known as Avicenna, a Persian scholar who lived 980-1037, and his tutor [al-Natili](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Natili). Ibn Sina and al-Natili are in my line of academic advisors, so I'm tracing the path from him to me (or me back to him). Most people with a PhD can probably do the same.

I am listing PhD advisors, or if a person has no PhD the advisor of the last graduate degree they received, or of the nearest dissertation equivalent. I also list the university or nearest equivalent where degree was achieved. Many people had multiple advisors, but to avoid an exponential explosion of advisors, I'm only tracking one advisor, chosen to reach Ibn Sina. There are many paths that reach him, and I chose arbitrarily amongst those paths. For each person, I list their name, the university at which they received their degree, degree year, years of birth and death, a link to their Mathematics Genealogy page, and the field that their degree is associated with.

Note that some of these links are a bit uncertain, especially from 1296 and earlier.

## Reflection

It's fun to watch the progression of places the ancestry goes through, as we walk back in time, reflecting different centers of academics at different times:
* United States, 1911-
* Germany, 1684-1911
* Switzerland, 1569-1684
* Italy, 1472-1569
* Byzantine Empire, 1300-1472
* Iran, Iraq, Turkmenistan, Ilkhanate, 990-1300

Note that these terms don't necessarily reflect what that area would have been called at the time.

The progression of areas is fun as well, reflected the most prestigious fields of research of the time. Computer Science, Mathematics, Medicine, Classics, Philosophy & Theology, Astronomy, and spreading out into polymaths familiar with many subjects.
